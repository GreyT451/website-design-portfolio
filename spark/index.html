Spark - Website Redesign & Mobile UI

Spark is a platform for entrepreneurs to grow and fund their business all in one place. The platform needed a design foundation: a website that communicated the product clearly, a system that could scale, and a first look at what a mobile experience could be.

Summary

Spark had grown organically without a defined design system. Every page had been designed in isolation, different visual and type treatments, inconsistent spacing, no shared components. The landing page buried the product's value under feature lists, and the dashboard organized content around internal product categories rather than what users actually came to do.

On the mobile side, no one had explored what a dedicated app experience should look like. The team needed something concrete to react to, a first- pass design that they could test internally and help them evaluate whether a mobile launch made sense and what it would take to execute it well.

The Approach

I started with an audit of the existing product to identify pain points and inconsistencies, mapping user flows, reviewing comparable platforms for structural reference and defining the information architecture. It showed user flows that shared zero UI patterns, every page had been built in isolation. The bulk of the work was iterative design: wireframing, building high-fidelity mockups, and assembling a component library. The final phase involved refinement and handoff to the development team. Designs were built and reviewed in a staging environment, which allowed the dev team to pressure-test components before committing to production.

Design Decisions

Dashboard and funding tracker structure

The dashboard was the first thing users saw after logging in. The original layout organized content around product features Services, Blog, Documents rather than around what users actually came to do. People logged in to check their funding and figure out what to do next, not to browse a feature menu. I restructured around those two actions: Funding Tracker at the top, Things To Do right below it, everything else in the sidebar.

Typography hierarchy

Established a clear type scale using Satoshi as the primary font. The hierarchy ranges from Display XL (88px) down to Body SM (13px), with consistent letter spacing and line-height ratios across every level. The primary brand color was changed, and I built everything around that color to let the accent do its job: highlight actions, not compete with content.

Reusable component library

Built 40+ components in Figma including buttons, inputs, cards, modals, and navigation, all with variants and states. This allowed the dev team to work faster and ensured design consistency across every page.

Mobile-first navigation

For the mobile experience, the tabs prioritized the three things users would reach for most: dashboard, funding tracker, and account settings. Everything else lived one level deeper. For an exploratory design meant to test feasibility, keeping navigation familiar and minimal was more useful than trying to innovate on patterns.

Upsell modal, flow & placements

The major part of redesign was the upselling modals. The product had multiple subscription tiers - plus, silver, gold, platinum. As part of the redesign, I renamed them to Free, Plus, Premium, and VIP. Clearer labels that made the value progression immediately readable. The challenge was deciding when to surface upgrade prompts, too early feels like a paywall, too late and users never discover the value of upgrading. We mapped moments where users were most likely to feel the limit of their current plan: after viewing a funding opportunity they couldn't fully access, after landing on the dashboard with locked features visible, and within the Concierge service which was gated by tier. Upgrade nudges were placed at those specific points, framed around what the user would gain rather than what they were missing.

Given more time, I would have validated these placements against actual user behavior in Google Analytics to confirm whether the nudges were appearing at genuine friction points or just assumed ones.

Learnings

01

Constraints sharpen the work

Locked brand colors, and working with two person design team setup pushed me to make deliberate decisions. Some of the cleanest outcomes came from having fewer options, not more.

03

New features need clear context

Designing the payment flow and upsell modals worked because I connected them to the overall user journey not as standalone features. A modal that appears after a user hits a plan limit feels like a natural next step. The same modal on the homepage feels like a paywall.

02

Audit before you design

Spending time upfront to map out pain points, user flows, and inconsistencies gave me a clear direction that i could defend. It kept me from redesigning things that didn't need to change.

04

What I'd do differently

I would have pushed for more dirct user feedback during the redesign. Working primarily from audit data and team reviews gave strong direction, but direct user input earlier would have added more confidence to decisions I was making on instinct and secondhand information. I'd also want analytics access post-launch to validate whether the information architecture changes actually shifted user behavior the way I expected.
