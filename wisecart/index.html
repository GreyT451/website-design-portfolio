Wise Cart

A shopping cart that reads the basket as a whole, while you shop, not per item after the fact.

Groceries decide diets, and nobody reads the whole basket

Nutrition advice happens per item: labels, apps, scores. But nobody eats one item. The unit that actually decides a week of eating is the full cart, and no tool reads it while the decisions are still being made.

  • 01

    Unhealthy food is the convenient default

    Unhealthy options are often cheaper, faster and easier to reach than the alternatives.

  • 02

    Processed food is marketed hardest

    The heaviest advertising budgets sit behind processed products, which keeps them the most visible choice in the store.

  • 03

    Nutritional value is opaque at the shelf

    Most shoppers cannot judge the nutritional weight of a full basket from individual labels while standing in an aisle.

A cart that shows the balance of everything in it

Wise Cart gives the shopper one live signal: how balanced the whole basket is right now. It is framed as balance across food groups, not as good and bad food, so the cart informs without shaming. Checkout happens on the cart itself. No register lane, no bagging station.

  • Scanner at the base of the handle

    Items are scanned one motion before they drop into the basket.

  • LED strip along the handle

    Shifts along a color scale as the basket fills, reading balance across food groups.

  • Weight sensing under the basket

    Confirms each scanned item by weight and flags anything that entered unscanned.

Why scan plus weight

The sensing approach was chosen by elimination, and the rejected options are part of the design.

Rejected

Camera detection of the full basket

Items in a cart stack and occlude each other, unlike items passing over a flat scanning surface. Vision alone cannot see what the pile is hiding.

Rejected

Lidar

Disproportionate hardware for the job. The cart needs to know what went in, not a point cloud of it.

Chosen

Scan plus weight

The scanner records intent, the scale verifies it. Two cheap sensors that check each other.

The approach has commercial precedent. In 2024 Amazon pulled its ceiling camera based Just Walk Out system from its full size grocery stores and moved to smart carts that scan and weigh at the basket.

Process

Drag or scroll sideways

Checking it at hand scale

Cardboard and print mockups settled the handle width, the button spacing and how the basket splits before any surface modelling was trusted.

The strip reads the whole cart

The LED strip along the handle is the entire interface while shopping. It shifts along a color scale as items go in, reading the balance of the basket across food groups. A full spectrum means a balanced cart. It is a reading, not a verdict: no food is marked bad, the cart only shows what the mix currently is.

  • Balanced cart
  • Meat and seafood
  • Fruits and vegetables
  • Dairy
  • Fats, sugar and oil

Scan, shop, check out

Pair the app with a tap, then scan each item at the handle as it goes in. Weight confirms it landed.

The strip shifts as the basket fills. The screen keeps a running total and can suggest swaps and offers along the way.

Pay on the cart and leave. No register lane, no bagging station, no second handling of the basket.

The business case

Manufacturing

Estimated cost lands around $131.5 per cart at mass production, hardware and raw material included.

Market

The smart cart market was valued at roughly $1.4 billion in 2022 and is projected to reach roughly $9.7 billion by 2030 (ResearchAndMarkets, 2023).

Who it serves
  • Shoppers

    Balance reading while shopping and checkout without a lane.

  • Retailers

    Replaces register lanes and surfaces offers at the moment of choice.

  • Point of sale companies

    Their payment hardware rides on every cart instead of one lane.

  • Brands

    Placement and offers inside the shopping moment itself.
