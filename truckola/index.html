01The Brief
ClientTruckola Technologies
ScopeVendor app redesign
Year2020
DurationFour months

Truckola Technologies is an Indian logistics startup connecting cargo clients to truck vendors. The vendor app already had every function the business needed — load discovery, trip management, document upload, billing, payment. But most vendors bypassed it entirely. They called Truckola's sales agents instead, who would walk them through each step, fill in details on their behalf, and confirm trips by phone.

The cost of this pattern sat on Truckola. The company ran a call center to compensate for an app vendors did not trust, and that headcount scaled linearly with the platform. The brief was to redesign the vendor experience so the app could carry its own weight, reduce call volume into the sales floor, and let the business grow without the call center growing with it.

The work happened inside the engineering team. Designs moved from sketch to implementation in the same room, with developers shipping new flows into staging while the next set was still on paper.

02Context

The Indian trucking market does not behave like a Western logistics market.

India's logistics sector was valued at 150 billion USD in 2020 and contributed about 14 percent of GDP. The vast majority of cargo moved by road. But the industry was, and largely still is, fragmented and underdigitised. Most vendors operated as individual owners or small family businesses.

Cash transactions dominated. Carbon-copy receipts were trusted more than digital documents. Drivers needed physical money for food, fuel, and lodging across multi-day journeys. Trust between strangers was thin, and deals were considered not done until cargo was physically loaded.

This context shaped every interface decision that followed. A design that assumed the conventions of an Uber or a DoorDash user would have failed immediately.

03System Mapping

Three actors, one transaction, dozens of handoffs.

Before any interface work could begin, the existing transaction had to be drawn out end to end. A single completed trip involved a client posting a load, the company sourcing vendors, a vendor accepting and dispatching a truck, a driver executing the trip, proof of delivery flowing back upstream, and payment routing through the company before reaching the vendor's wallet. Every step touched at least two actors. Every handoff was a place the system could leak.

Fig. 03Process flowClient, Vendor, and Company swimlanes showing the complete trip lifecycle. Source: pages 35–36.

Mapping the system this way made the failure pattern visible. Vendors were not avoiding the app because it was hard to use in isolation. They were avoiding it because the app sat in the middle of a chain that depended on trust — and the call agents had become the trust layer the interface had never managed to be.

04Audit

A feature-complete app that nobody wanted to use.

The existing app already did everything the business needed — vendors could discover loads, accept trips, upload proof of delivery, confirm freight memos, track payments, and manage their fleet. The problem was not absence. Every one of those flows had quietly accumulated enough small frictions that calling a sales agent was always the faster option.

The audit used Jakob Nielsen's ten heuristics, run with four evaluators, supplemented with task mapping across the full vendor journey. Twenty-seven distinct issues surfaced across the ten heuristics. None of them on their own were catastrophic. Together they explained why the call center existed.

Where it broke

Vocabulary.

Short forms like POD, FM, and LR were used throughout without explanation. Most vendors did not know what they stood for. The interface assumed a language the audience did not speak.

Trust.

There was no in-app help, no FAQ, no visible loading states, and no clear way to recover from a mistake. When something went wrong, vendors defaulted to calling the company instead of trying again.

Density.

Crucial information sat in tiny type. Multiple buttons led to the same destination. Tab structures forced users to swipe through long lists when filtering would have served them better.

05Research

Thirteen conversations across four cities.

Research involved in-person and phone interviews with thirteen people: vendors, fleet owners, transport company managers, employees, and two of Truckola's own call agents in the sales department. Locations spanned Bhiwandi, Vashi, Thane, Kanpur, and Haryana.

It is much easier if we get on the call with the person at the company.
Vendor Manager · Bhiwandi

The app was not failing because the interface was ugly. It was failing because the calling relationship felt human and the app did not. Vendors trusted a voice on the phone in a way no progress bar could replace. The redesign had to acknowledge that and quietly earn ground back — not try to replace the call agents overnight.

06The Core Problem

The call center had become the interface.

Across thirteen interviews one pattern repeated. Vendors did not see the app as a tool. They saw it as a thing they were sometimes asked to acknowledge after the real transaction had already happened on the phone. Loads were discussed, negotiated, and verbally confirmed with a sales agent. The app was where, eventually, the paperwork caught up.

This was efficient for the vendor in the short term and expensive for Truckola at every scale. Each onboarded vendor created a permanent call obligation. Each new trip produced multiple inbound calls: one to confirm the load, one to clarify an address, one to ask about payment status, one to walk through document upload. The sales floor was effectively a human API for an app that vendors did not feel they could operate alone.

The redesign could not remove the call center. The trust it provided was real and earned. But it could shift the relationship: route the truly ambiguous moments to a human, and let the app handle everything that did not actually need a voice. The goal was not zero calls. The goal was calls that mattered.

07Synthesis

Three vendor archetypes shaped the work.

Archetype 01
Owner / Driver
VehiclesOne
ExperienceEight years

Owns and drives a single truck, often the sole earner for the family. Wants steady loads and predictable payouts. Most frustrated when payment cycles slip and he has no clear way to chase status without leaving a phone call.

Archetype 02
Fleet Owner
VehiclesSix to twenty
ExperienceFifteen years

Runs a small fleet with hired drivers and a clerk. Wants visibility across multiple trucks at once and the ability to delegate without losing oversight. Most frustrated when documents and bills live across separate threads he cannot reconcile.

Archetype 03
Transport Company
VehiclesThirty or more
ExperienceTwenty years

Manages a full back office with dedicated coordinators. Wants the app to behave as an extension of their existing logistics workflow, not a parallel system. Most frustrated when the platform forces them to duplicate work they already do in ledgers.

‹ ›

The revised architecture collapsed repeated tabs across the home and load screens, surfaced primary call-to-action buttons where icons had previously stood alone, and brought contact and help options up out of buried profile menus. The goal was to reduce the number of taps required for the three most frequent tasks — checking new loads, confirming a trip, and uploading delivery documents — and to make the rest of the app's depth optional rather than confrontational.

09The Work

Quiet, legible, and built to be trusted.

The design principles that came out of the research were modest: clear typographic hierarchy in Roboto, generous spacing, plain language wherever a short form had previously stood, explicit confirmation states for irreversible actions, a call button always one tap away from any trip screen, and consistent placement of map and address blocks across every screen that touched a trip. Every decision had the same underlying test — would a confident vendor still need to call a sales agent to complete this step? If the answer was yes, the screen was not done.

11Companion Work

A second app for the other side of the platform.

The internship also included a parallel three-month group project: a cargo client mobile app built around the existing Truckola website. The brief was similar in shape but the user was different. Clients were more comfortable with digital tools, and the design could move faster and lean on more conventional patterns.

12In Retrospect

What this project taught me.

This project was my first sustained encounter with research as a design discipline. The heuristic evaluation, the persona work, the system mapping, and the rhythm of letting interviews actually change the design rather than confirming what I already wanted to do are all still recognisable in how I approach problems today.

The brief was operational. The intent was to reduce Truckola's dependency on a call center that had become the de facto interface for the vendor side of the platform. I do not have the post-launch metrics to know how much that shifted. What I do know is that the team I worked with shipped this redesign, that I watched flows move from paper to staging in the same week, and that the discipline of designing against a measurable business pressure rather than against an aesthetic ideal is the thing I carry from this project most.

Some of the visual decisions I would now make differently. The reliance on Material Design as a system was the right pragmatic choice in 2020 for an Indian Android-first audience, but the result lacks the kind of identity I would now want to build for a project at this scale. What holds up is the research rigor, the systems thinking, and the discipline of designing for the specific context I was in rather than the conventions of a market I was not.
